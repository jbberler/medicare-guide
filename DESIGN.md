## Problem Statement

When people turn 65, they must decide whether and how to enroll in Medicare. This is a high-stakes, long-duration decision that most people make with low confidence. The existing options — Google (overwhelming), brokers (too heavy, sales-driven), Medicare.gov Plan Finder (broken, can't compare Medigap vs. MA), ChatGPT (can't handle personalization) — all fail because they either provide generic education OR personalized recommendations, but never both.

The founder's parents are actively stuck on this decision right now, with a few-month deadline. They tried three channels and all failed. The core insight: they don't just want to be told what to do — they need to understand their situation well enough to trust the recommendation.

## Demand Evidence

- Founder's parents are going through this decision RIGHT NOW with a hard deadline
- They tried Google → overwhelmed by the four parts of Medicare
- They tried friends → got conflicting advice
- They tried ChatGPT → confusing because it couldn't map their specific financial situation to a clear recommendation
- They are still stuck after trying all three channels
- The specific complexity: income over $206K triggers IRMAA surcharges, one spouse has employer insurance, they want to see specific doctors — "just sign up for Medicare" is wrong advice for them

## Status Quo

The current workaround is "muddle through with Google and hope you don't screw it up." Specifically:

- **Brokers:** Exist but are "too heavy a lift." High friction, feels like hiring a professional for a personal decision. Commission incentives (especially toward Medicare Advantage) erode trust.
- **Medicare.gov Plan Finder:** GAO report found 3/4 of SHIP counselors say beneficiaries can't navigate it. ProPublica investigation called it "the $11M tool that gives seniors wrong information." Cannot compare Medigap vs. Medicare Advantage — the most important comparison.
- **SHIP counselors:** Free and good but overloaded and hard to access.
- **ChatGPT/AI chatbots:** Have the information but fail at personalization. No structure, no guided journey, no memory of what you don't know.

The gap: **personalized guidance that builds understanding, without the friction of engaging a broker or the confusion of unstructured research.**

## Target User & Narrowest Wedge

**Primary user:** A couple where one spouse is turning 65, the other may be different age. One spouse still works and has employer/private insurance. Household income over $206K (IRMAA applies). Generally healthy but want provider choice — they want to see their specific doctors.

**Why this wedge:** This is the case where the decision is genuinely complex. The standard "just sign up" advice is wrong. IRMAA makes Medicare expensive. Employer insurance may be the better deal. Two spouses may need different strategies. Generic tools can't handle this.

**Narrowest v1:** Married couple, age 64-66, one spouse employed with group insurance, income near or above IRMAA threshold. One flow, one persona, one decision.

**v1 scope on spouse divergence:** v1 treats the turning-65 spouse as the primary decision-maker. The flow collects information about the other spouse's coverage status but produces one decision memo for the person turning 65. If the other spouse is also near 65, the tool suggests running through the flow again for them. Fully interleaved couple strategies are v2.

## Constraints

- Medicare rules change annually — 2026 has significant changes ($2,100 OOP cap, $35 insulin, negotiated drug prices). CMS publishes new rates each October/November for the following year.
- IRMAA brackets use income from 2 years prior (2024 tax return → 2026 premiums)
- Must be accurate — wrong Medicare advice has real financial consequences (late enrollment penalties are permanent)
- No carrier integrations needed for v1 — hard-coded 2026 tables suffice
- No login/accounts needed for v1 — privacy-preserving, all data stays in browser, nothing sent to server
- Accessibility: WCAG 2.1 AA compliance, mobile-first design, minimum 16px body text, high contrast — target users are 64-66 years old
- Annual rate updates require a code change and redeployment. Consider a JSON file in a public repo to allow non-engineer updates in the future.

## Premises

1. The core problem is confidence through comprehension, not just getting a recommendation. Users need to understand WHY the recommendation is right for them.
2. The target user is a higher-income couple ($200K+) with employer insurance — the complex cases where generic advice fails.
3. A TurboTax-style guided experience that teaches each concept before asking for input solves this better than a chatbot, calculator, or content site.
4. (Revised) The tool builds understanding and produces a decision memo; the endgame is confidence to validate with a human (SHIP counselor), not to replace the human entirely.

## Cross-Model Perspective

Codex (independent cold read, no conversation context):

- **Steelman:** "TurboTax for Medicare timing and coverage decisions" — not a content site and not a plan search tool. Takes household facts, models decisions that materially change cost/risk, explains the result well enough to act with confidence.
- **Key insight:** "ChatGPT (confusing — couldn't handle the personalization)" is the clearest signal. Product should be a structured, rules-driven decision flow with auditable scenario outputs — NOT a chatbot.
- **Challenged Premise 4:** The tool may make people understand but not confident enough to act. Evidence: if users still refuse to enroll without human confirmation, the tool is an adviser, not the final decider. → **Accepted and revised:** decision memo + SHIP counselor handoff is the right endgame.
- **48-hour prototype:** Next.js + TypeScript + Tailwind + Zod on Vercel. Plain TS rules engine with hard-coded 2026 Medicare/IRMAA tables. 6-8 step wizard → 3 scenario outputs → printable decision memo.

## Approaches Considered

### Approach A: The Decision Memo (MVP)
- 6-8 step wizard collecting household facts → 3 side-by-side scenarios → printable decision memo
- Ships fastest (~45 min Claude Code time), directly buildable from Codex's prototype spec
- **Weakness:** Doesn't build deep understanding. Explains results but not concepts. Misses Premise 1.

### Approach B: The Guided Journey (TurboTax model) — CHOSEN
- Step-by-step experience that teaches each concept BEFORE asking for input
- "Here's how IRMAA works for your income level" → "Now enter your income"
- Each step builds understanding, culminating in personalized recommendation + printable decision memo
- ~1.5 hours Claude Code time. Directly addresses Premise 1 (confidence through comprehension).
- **Weakness:** More content to write, longer user flow. But the educational layer IS the differentiation.

### Approach C: The Scenario Playground
- Interactive dashboard where users toggle variables and see costs change in real-time
- Powerful for exploration but no guidance — overwhelms the same users who got lost on Google
- Doesn't solve "I don't know what I don't know"
- **Rejected:** Fails for the same reason ChatGPT fails — power without structure.

## Recommended Approach

**Approach B: The Guided Journey.** The educational layer is what makes this product different from everything else in the landscape. The delta between A and B is ~45 minutes of Claude Code time. Your parents said they need to understand it to trust it — Approach B is the only one that addresses that directly.

### App Shell & Layout Architecture

**Desktop (≥768px):** Two-zone layout.
- **Left rail (280px fixed):** Progress tracker showing all 8 steps (current step highlighted, completed steps show green checkmarks). Below the progress tracker: a "Running Summary" panel that accumulates the user's key inputs as they complete each step (e.g., "Age: 65 · Income: $206K-$258K · Employer coverage: Yes"). This keeps context visible as later steps reference earlier inputs.
- **Main pane (fluid):** Active step content — educational panel + input fields.
- **Transition:** When advancing steps, the main pane content transitions with a subtle left-to-right slide (150ms ease-out). No page reload.

**Mobile (<768px):** Single-column with persistent context.
- **Sticky header (56px):** Step indicator dots (like iOS onboarding) + current step label. Tapping the dots opens a full-screen progress overlay showing all steps and the running summary.
- **Main content:** Active step (educational content + inputs), full-width.
- **Bottom sheet (collapsible):** Running summary accessible via a pull-up handle. Collapsed by default, showing one line ("3 of 8 completed").

**Navigation:** Back and forward buttons at the bottom of the main pane. Back button always available after Step 1. Forward button disabled until required fields pass validation. Keyboard: Enter advances, Escape goes back.

### Core Flow (8 steps)

Each step follows the **teach-then-ask** pattern: an educational panel appears above the input fields in the main pane. The educational content varies in format to prevent "wallpaper" fatigue (see Educational Content Formats below). Approximate copy length: 30-60 words for most steps, up to 100 words for the IRMAA step which requires more explanation.

**Educational Content Formats (vary by step to maintain engagement):**
- **One-line rule summary** with an expandable "Why this matters" block (Steps 2, 3)
- **Mini number example** showing a specific calculation ("If your AGI is $250K, your Part B premium is $X/mo instead of $185/mo") (Step 4)
- **Deadline strip** — a timeline graphic showing key dates and penalty windows (Step 6)
- **Comparison snippet** — a 2-row mini-table contrasting two options inline (Step 5)

**Age gate:** If the primary user enters an age under 65, replace the step content (not a modal) with an interstitial: "Medicare eligibility begins at 65. This tool is designed for people turning 65 soon. If you're planning ahead, you can still explore — but enrollment actions won't apply yet." Two buttons: "Continue exploring" (appends a persistent yellow banner to all subsequent steps: "You're under 65 — exploring ahead of your eligibility window") and "Exit" (returns to Welcome).

1. **Welcome** — Hero: a visual "Medicare Decision Map" showing the flow: Current Coverage → Your Age → Your Income → Your Recommendation. One CTA: "Build my decision memo →". Below: brief expectations (15-20 minutes, personalized output, no data leaves your browser). The decision map IS the brand — it immediately communicates what this tool does and why it's different from a generic Medicare article.
2. **Your household** — collect: ages (both spouses), sex (for Medigap premium lookup — uses CMS terminology "sex at birth"), marital status, state of residence. Teach: why age and state matter (Medicare eligibility at 65, Medigap premiums vary by state and sex, some states have guaranteed-issue protections).
3. **Your insurance** — collect: who has employer coverage (you, your spouse, or both), current coverage type (employer group, COBRA, ACA, none), employer size (20+ employees?), monthly premium, who is covered. Teach: how employer insurance interacts with Medicare — the Medicare Secondary Payer rules differ based on who holds the policy. If the turning-65 person's employer has 20+ employees, employer plan is primary and Part B can be deferred penalty-free. If coverage is through a spouse's employer, the same 20+ rule applies but through the spouse's employer.
4. **Your income** — collect: IRMAA bracket via dropdown (ranges, not exact figures), expected income change (retiring within 12 months?). Teach: IRMAA explained — Medicare uses your tax return from 2 years ago, higher income = higher premiums, and you can appeal (SSA-44 form) if income drops due to a qualifying life event like retirement.
5. **Your health** — collect: general health (healthy / managing conditions / frequent care), current medications (none / few generics / specialty drugs), provider preference (specific doctors important? yes/no). Teach: how networks differ — Original Medicare lets you see any Medicare-accepting doctor nationwide, Medicare Advantage restricts to plan networks, Medigap supplements Original Medicare with predictable costs.
6. **Your timeline** — collect: planned retirement date (or already retired), when employer coverage ends. "Retiring soon" is defined as **within 12 months**. Teach: Special Enrollment Period rules — you have 8 months after employer coverage ends to enroll in Part B without penalty. Missing this window means a permanent 10% surcharge per year you were late.
7. **Your scenarios** — no input, output only. Presented as a comparison table (desktop) or tabbed view (mobile). Between Step 6 and Step 7, show a 2-second "Analyzing your situation..." loading interstitial with a summary of key inputs being processed. This is a trust mechanism — instant results feel like templates.
8. **Your decision memo** — Transitional "Your memo is ready" screen with a plain-language summary of the recommendation before offering print/save. Then: printable summary generated entirely in-browser via CSS print styles + `window.print()`. No server involved.

**Desktop layout:** A comparison table with rows and 3 columns. The recommended scenario column has a subtle highlight (light background tint + "Best fit" badge).

|---|---|---|---|
| Monthly cost | $0 (Part A) + employer premium | Part B + IRMAA + Medigap + Part D | Part B + IRMAA + MA premium |
| Annual total | [calculated] | [calculated] | [calculated] |
| IRMAA impact | None | Full (Part B + Part D) | Part B only |
| Doctor freedom | Current network only | Any Medicare doctor nationwide | Plan network only |
| Penalty risk | None (employer exemption) | None if enrolling during IEP/SEP | None if enrolling during IEP/SEP |

Below the table: one recommendation panel with the "Best fit" scenario's rationale in a highlighted box.

**Mobile layout:** Tab bar with "A: Employer" / "B: Medicare + Medigap" / "C: Advantage" tabs. Each tab shows one scenario's full breakdown. Sticky comparison bar at bottom showing all three monthly costs simultaneously so the user can compare while viewing details.

**Scenario details:**

**Scenario A: Stay on employer insurance, take free Part A only**
- Monthly cost: $0 (Part A is free with 40+ work credits; if <40 credits, Part A has a premium — noted with disclaimer) + existing employer premium
- IRMAA impact: None (no Part B = no surcharge)
- Provider access: Keep current doctors and network
- Penalty risk: None (employer coverage exempts you from late-enrollment penalty)
- Best when: Employer plan is good, income is high (IRMAA avoidance), not retiring within 12 months

**Scenario B: Enroll in Original Medicare (Parts A + B) + Medigap + Part D**
- Monthly cost: Part B premium (with IRMAA tier) + Medigap Plan G premium (by age/sex/state) + Part D premium (with IRMAA)
- IRMAA impact: Full — both Part B and Part D surcharges apply
- Provider access: Any Medicare-accepting doctor nationwide
- Penalty risk: None if enrolling during IEP or SEP
- Best when: Retiring soon, want broadest provider choice, value predictable costs

**Scenario C: Enroll in Medicare Advantage (Part C)**
- Monthly cost: Part B premium (with IRMAA) + MA plan premium ($0-50 typical)
- IRMAA impact: Part B surcharge still applies
- Provider access: Plan network only — must verify specific doctors are in-network
- Penalty risk: None if enrolling during IEP or SEP
- Best when: Lower premiums are priority, willing to accept network restrictions, OK with prior authorization requirements

### Rules Engine — High-Level Decision Logic

```
INPUT: age, spouse_age, sex, state,
       coverage_type (enum: "employer_group" | "cobra" | "aca" | "none"),
       employer_holder (enum: "me" | "spouse" | "both" — only if coverage_type == "employer_group"),
       employer_size (20+? — only if coverage_type == "employer_group"),
       employer_premium (monthly — only if coverage_type != "none"),
       irmaa_bracket,
       retiring_soon (bool — within 12 months), retirement_date,
       health_status, has_specific_doctors (bool), medications_level

IRMAA LOOKUP:
  irmaa_bracket → Part B monthly premium (from 2026 CMS table)
  irmaa_bracket → Part D monthly surcharge (from 2026 CMS table)

MEDIGAP LOOKUP:
  age + sex + state → Medigap Plan G estimated premium (from state median tables)
  NOTE: v1 uses median estimates by age band, sex, and state. Disclaimer on all
  Medigap figures: "Estimate based on state medians. Actual quotes vary by insurer.
  Contact a SHIP counselor or insurer for exact pricing."

COBRA/ACA GATE (check FIRST, before scenario ranking):
  IF coverage_type == "COBRA" OR coverage_type == "ACA":
    → NEVER recommend Scenario A
    → Show prominent warning: "⚠️ COBRA/ACA coverage is NOT creditable for Medicare.
       Delaying Part B enrollment while on COBRA/ACA will result in a permanent
       late-enrollment penalty (10% surcharge per year of delay)."
    → Skip to Scenario B vs C comparison below
    → Add to decision memo action items: "Enroll in Part B immediately — your
       COBRA/ACA coverage does not protect you from late-enrollment penalties."

SCENARIO RANKING:
  IF employer_coverage AND employer_size >= 20 AND NOT retiring_soon
      AND coverage_type != "COBRA" AND coverage_type != "ACA":
    → Tag Scenario A as "Best fit for your situation"
    → Reason: IRMAA avoidance saves $X/year, no penalty risk with creditable coverage
  ELIF retiring_soon OR NOT employer_coverage OR coverage_type IN ("COBRA", "ACA"):
    IF has_specific_doctors:
      → Tag Scenario B as "Best fit for your situation"
      → Reason: Broadest provider access, predictable costs
    ELSE:
      → Compare: IF (Scenario B monthly total - Scenario C monthly total) > $150/mo:
          Tag Scenario C as "Best fit for cost savings"
        ELSE:
          Tag Scenario B as "Best fit overall"
          (When the cost difference is small, broader access wins)
  ELSE:
    → No "best fit" tag. Show all three with explanation:
      "Your situation has trade-offs in multiple directions. Review all three
       and discuss with your SHIP counselor."

  Always show all three scenarios regardless of recommendation.
```

**Validation error tone examples:**
- Instead of: "Invalid age" → Use: "Medicare eligibility starts at 65. Enter your age as of this year."
- Instead of: "Required field" → Use: "We need this to calculate your options — it stays in your browser."
- Instead of: "Invalid income bracket" → Use: "Pick the income range closest to your 2024 tax return — you can adjust this later."

**Mid-flow persistence (localStorage):**
- Auto-save all inputs on each step completion to localStorage.
- On return visit: auto-resume at last completed step with a banner: "Welcome back — your information is saved locally. Pick up where you left off." Two actions: "Continue" (resumes) and "Start over" (clears localStorage).
- localStorage data expires after 30 days.
- Clear localStorage when the user prints/downloads the decision memo (journey complete).

**Name field:** Add an optional first-name field to Step 1 (Welcome), below the decision map CTA. Label: "What should we call you? (optional — stays in your browser)". Used in the memo header. If blank, memo reads "Your Medicare Decision Memo" instead of "Medicare Decision Memo — Prepared for [Name]".

### Input Validation (Zod schemas)

- All fields required except retirement_date (only required if retiring_soon = true) and name (always optional)
- Age: 62-70 (reasonable window around Medicare eligibility; ages 62-64 trigger age-gate interstitial)
- Sex: enum ("Male", "Female", "Prefer not to say") — "Prefer not to say" defaults to higher premium estimate (Female) with note: "We'll use the higher estimate to give you a conservative cost picture."
- Income bracket: enum of IRMAA tiers (dropdown, not free text)
- Employer size: boolean (20+ or not — this is the Medicare Secondary Payer threshold)
- Employer holder: enum ("me", "spouse", "both") — determines MSP rules
- State: US state enum (for Medigap premium lookup). States outside the curated 10-15 show a notice after selection: "We don't have Medigap estimates for [State] yet. You'll still get scenarios A and C with full numbers, and Scenario B with all details except the Medigap premium."
- Inline validation with warm, guiding error messages (see tone examples above)

### Decision Memo Structure

The printable memo contains:
1. **Header:** "Medicare Decision Memo — Prepared for [Name] · [Date]"
2. **Situation summary:** 2-3 sentences recapping their key inputs (age, income tier, coverage status)
3. **Recommendation:** Which scenario and why (1 paragraph)
4. **Cost comparison table:** All three scenarios side-by-side with monthly and annual totals
5. **Action items checklist:** 4-6 concrete next steps with checkboxes (e.g., "Enroll in Part A at ssa.gov," "Request Certificate of Creditable Coverage from employer")
6. **Key deadlines:** Initial Enrollment Period dates, SEP window if applicable
7. **What you learned:** Bullet list of concepts covered during the journey
8. **Footer:** "Bring this memo to your SHIP counselor for a free 15-minute review. Find yours at shiphelp.org"

Generated entirely client-side via CSS `@media print` styles and `window.print()`. No server-side PDF generation. All data stays in the browser.

### Tech Stack

- Next.js + TypeScript + Tailwind + Zod on Vercel
- Plain TypeScript rules engine — no LLM dependency for recommendations
- Hard-coded 2026 Medicare/IRMAA/premium tables in JSON (sourced from CMS.gov and SSA.gov)
- Medigap premium estimates by age band, sex, and state (see Data Preparation below)
- No login, no accounts, no server-side data storage — privacy by default
- CSS print styles for decision memo output
- Mobile-first responsive design, WCAG 2.1 AA compliant

### Data Preparation (non-trivial)

Medigap premium tables by age band, sex, and state are **not available as a single machine-readable source**. State insurance departments publish rate filings per-insurer, per-state.

**v1 approach:** Limit to a curated set of 10-15 states with the most accessible rate data (start with the founder's parents' state). Use median Plan G premiums across top 5 insurers in each state, bucketed by age (65, 66-70, 71-75) and sex. Display a prominent disclaimer: "Estimate based on state medians across major insurers. Your actual quote may be 20-40% higher or lower. Contact a SHIP counselor for exact pricing."

**v2:** Expand to all 50 states as rate data is collected. Consider partnering with a data aggregator or scraping state insurance commission public filings.

## Open Questions

1. **Content accuracy:** Who reviews the Medicare content for accuracy? The founder has domain proximity (parents going through it) but should have a SHIP counselor or licensed Medicare specialist review before launch.
2. **Annual updates:** Medicare rules change every year. CMS publishes new rates each October/November. The JSON tables need updating and redeploying each fall. A "Last updated: [date] for [year] rates" banner should be visible in the app.
3. **Scope expansion:** When to expand beyond the high-income-employer-insurance wedge to serve lower-income users, people without employer coverage, or people on COBRA/ACA?
4. **Monetization:** This solves a real problem but the user is in a one-time decision window. Potential models: referral to vetted SHIP counselors, annual review reminders, expansion to other enrollment periods (OEPMA, AEP).

## Success Criteria

1. A user matching the target persona (high-income couple, employer insurance, turning 65) can complete the flow in 15-20 minutes
2. The decision memo is clear enough that a SHIP counselor can review it in 15 minutes and confirm or adjust the recommendation
3. The user understands WHY the recommendation is what it is — not just what to do, but why

## CEO Review Decisions

Decisions locked during `/plan-ceo-review` on 2026-03-23. Mode: HOLD SCOPE.

### Architecture

**Wizard state management:**
- React Context + useReducer + localStorage. Steps are Next.js App Router routes (`/steps/1` through `/steps/8`).
- Context is the source of truth — steps render from context state, never from route cache.
- Auto-save all inputs to localStorage on each step completion. On return visit: resume banner with "Continue" / "Start over" options. Data expires after 30 days and is cleared after memo print/download.

**Conditional invalidation on back-navigation:**
- When a user goes back and changes an upstream answer, clear dependent downstream fields and show a brief "answers updated" notice.
- Example: changing "employer coverage: yes → no" clears Step 6 (Timeline) fields.

**localStorage error handling** (`lib/storage.ts`):
- Wrap all localStorage calls in a try/catch utility.
- Quota exceeded → toast "Storage full — progress may not be saved between sessions", retry once.
- Private browsing / disabled → banner on app load: "Private browsing detected — your progress won't be saved if you close this tab." Wizard still works in-memory.
- Corrupted JSON → reset to empty state, show "Your saved progress was corrupted and has been cleared."

**Navigation:**
- Debounce Next/Back buttons 300ms to prevent double-click skipping (target users are 64-66).
- Keyboard: Enter advances, Escape goes back (per design doc — noted here for implementation).

### Rules Engine Additions

**Work credits field:**
- Add `has_40_credits: boolean` to Step 2 (Household): "Have you or your spouse worked 10+ years and paid Medicare taxes?"
- Add to Zod schema in `lib/schemas.ts`.
- Rules engine: if `has_40_credits = false`, Scenario A shows the 2026 Part A premium ($280/mo with 30-39 quarters, $518/mo with fewer) instead of $0. Add disclaimer: "Your Part A premium depends on your exact work history. Verify at ssa.gov."
- Unit tests to add: `has_40_credits false → Part A premium shown`, `has_40_credits true → Part A is $0`.

**Defensive lookups:**
- Every rules engine table lookup must have a typed fallback.
- For unsupported Medigap states: return `null`, render "Medigap estimate unavailable for [State] — get a free quote at shiphelp.org" inline in Scenario B. Card remains visible with all other fields.
- For IRMAA lookups (complete CMS data): assert key exists, throw a named `LookupError` if missing. Catch at the scenario rendering layer: show "Unable to calculate — please verify your inputs." (Should never trigger with valid Zod-validated inputs.)

### UX Decisions

**Graceful redirect for non-target users:**
- After Step 4 (Income), if `no employer coverage AND no IRMAA bracket`, show a redirect interstitial:
  - "Your situation is straightforward — here's what we recommend" with a brief summary (enroll in Parts A+B during IEP, consider Medigap, SHIP link).
  - Button: "Continue to full analysis anyway" (escape hatch).
  - If user continues: Scenario A shows "N/A — no employer coverage" instead of cost figures.

**IRMAA reassurance (Step 4):**
- Add one sentence at the bottom of the Step 4 IRMAA educational panel: *"You'll see exactly how this affects your options in Step 7 — and there may be a path that avoids this entirely."*
- Rationale: the IRMAA cost reveal ($340-$594/mo vs $185/mo) is a shock moment for the target persona. The forward-reference prevents abandonment without changing the educational content.

**Artificial loading delay (Step 7):**
- The "Analyzing your situation..." 2-second interstitial before Step 7 is an intentional trust mechanism, not real async work.
- Implement as `setTimeout(2000)`. Add a code comment: `// Intentional delay — instant results feel like templates. This signals personalization.`
- Do not remove thinking it is a bug.

### Security

- No analytics capturing form field values — income bracket and health status are sensitive.
- CSP headers: block inline scripts (except React hydration), no external script loading.
- All personalization is client-side only — no SSR of user data.
- SRI on any CDN-loaded assets (fonts, etc.).

### Observability

- No third-party error tracking for v1 (privacy-first — the "no data leaves your browser" promise).
- Add `console.error` in all defensive catch blocks for developer debugging.
- App footer: "Rates current for 2026 · Last updated March 2026" — makes data staleness visible to users.
- Post-MVP: add Sentry with `beforeSend` PII filtering if user base grows beyond known users.
